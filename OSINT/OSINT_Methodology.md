# OSINT Methodology
Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) methodology is a systematic process used to gather, analyze, and apply intelligence collected from publicly available sources. It is commonly employed in fields like cybersecurity, law enforcement, journalism, and corporate investigations. The methodology typically follows a structured sequence that ensures the information gathered is relevant, accurate, and useful.

The first step in OSINT methodology is planning and direction. This involves clearly defining the objective of the investigation or intelligence gathering effort. Practitioners identify key questions they want to answer and establish the scope of the project. At this stage, appropriate tools and platforms are selected to facilitate the collection process, such as search engines, metadata analysis tools, or specialized OSINT software like Maltego, Shodan, or Spiderfoot.

The next phase is collection, where data is gathered from a variety of open sources. These sources can include websites, social media platforms, public records, online forums, news articles, blogs, and even the deep or dark web. Information collected may be text, images, videos, or documents, depending on the nature of the investigation.

Once the data is collected, it undergoes processing. This step involves organizing and converting raw data into a usable format. It may include translating foreign-language content, structuring data into spreadsheets or databases, and removing duplicates or irrelevant information. The goal is to prepare the data for in-depth analysis.

The analysis phase is where insights are derived from the processed data. Analysts examine the data for patterns, connections, and trends. They validate the credibility of sources and use tools such as geolocation, link analysis, or timeline creation to better understand relationships and context. This step is crucial for drawing meaningful conclusions.

After the analysis, findings are compiled in the production phase. This involves creating a report or presentation that communicates the intelligence in a clear and actionable way. Reports typically include evidence, visual aids such as maps or graphs, and direct answers to the initial questions posed in the planning stage.

Finally, the dissemination and feedback phase involves sharing the intelligence with relevant stakeholders. This could be a client, supervisor, or investigative team. Feedback is gathered to evaluate the usefulness of the information and refine future OSINT cycles. This step ensures that OSINT practices remain effective and responsive to changing needs.
