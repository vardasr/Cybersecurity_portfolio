# Overview
In this lab, I will assume the role of digital forensics specialist who has been assigned to a case involving intellectual property theft. After obtaining a search warrant, the local authorities have seized multiple computers from the suspects and transferred images of their hard drives to the digital forensic team. So far, no incriminating evidence has been found on the drive images, but my boss, the lead investigator believes the suspects may have deleted incriminating evidence. As a digital forensics specialist, I will use professional data recovery tools and techniques to recover deleted data from different operating systems and file systems.
## Methods
* (Section 1 Part 1) I will recover deleted files from a Windows drive image.
* (Section 1 Part 2) I will recover deleted files from a Linux drive image.
* (Section2 Part 1) I will observe first-hand how easily a deleted file can be recovered.
* (Section 2 Part 2) I will use PhotoRec, a popular data carving utility, to recover data from a corrupted hard drive.
* (Section 3 Part 1) I will be using E3, to Import the FAT32.img drive image located in the Data Recovery Evidence Folder and attempt to recover at least one additional patent file.
* (Section 3 Part 2)  I will be using Autopsy, to Import the APFS.img drive image located in the Data Recovery Evidence folder and attempt to recover at least one additional patent file.
## Summary
For this lab I had three important concepts to learn. The first concept was recovering deleted files from an NTFS Drive Image with E3 and recovering deleted files from an Ext4 Drive Image with Autopsy. The second concept that I learned was in windows while using DiskDigger tool and in Linux using PhotoRec. The final concept I had to learn was puting this all together and finding the recovered data in the evidence folder.
